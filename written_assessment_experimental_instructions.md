## 1. Overview and Purpose

> **Grading:** This module uses the **Alternative Linear Conversion Grade Scale — 40% Pass (85% = A-)**. Your total percentage mark is converted to a UCD letter grade using this scale. Key boundaries: A- begins at 85%, B- at 70%, C- at 55%, D- (minimum pass) at 40%. Marks below 40% are fail grades. The full grade conversion table is published on the UCD Understanding Grades webpage.

> **Scope:** These instructions apply exclusively to **experimental summer research projects** — projects that involve laboratory work including, but not limited to, cell culture, bioprocessing, analytical assays, chromatography, formulation, and related experimental techniques in biopharmaceutical engineering. If your project is entirely computational (bioinformatics), refer to the separate bioinformatics assessment instructions.

Your written submission takes the form of a **full research thesis**, in accordance with the School of Chemical and Bioprocess Engineering Research Project Guidelines. This is the standard format for reporting experimental research at Master's level and reflects the depth of laboratory work you will have conducted over the course of your project.

**Why a longer format for experimental projects?** The thesis format (up to 20,000 words) is necessary because experimental research generates material that must be documented in full:

- **Materials and Methods** must be described in sufficient detail for another researcher to reproduce your experiments in the laboratory. This includes reagents, concentrations, equipment, protocols, culture conditions, analytical methods, and statistical approaches. This level of detail is essential for scientific reproducibility and cannot be condensed into a short communication.
- **Experimental optimisation** is a normal part of laboratory research. You may need to describe iterative steps — adjusting parameters, troubleshooting failed experiments, refining protocols — that are integral to your findings. This narrative demonstrates your practical scientific competence and problem-solving skills.
- **Negative and inconclusive results** are a legitimate and important part of experimental research. Experiments that did not produce the expected outcome still contribute to understanding. You are expected to report these honestly and discuss what they mean. Omitting unsuccessful experiments would misrepresent the work and violate scientific integrity.
- **Multiple results chapters** may be needed where your project addressed several related experimental questions or involved distinct phases of work.

Students on the bioinformatics cohort submit a shorter document (a research communication of 2,000–2,500 words) because computational work does not require the same level of procedural documentation and does not involve iterative laboratory optimisation. The difference is in format, not in academic standard — both cohorts are assessed with equivalent rigour and the same grade conversion scale.

**Why this project matters for your career.** Pharmaceutical and biopharmaceutical manufacturing are key sectors in the Irish and global economy, generating over 50 per cent of Irish GDP and providing excellent job opportunities for graduates. This module (CHEN40140) consolidates your taught coursework through intensive research experience, providing a route to technological publications and rounding off your year of study. The programme and its academic faculty are closely linked with the National Institute for Bioprocessing Research and Training (NIBRT), a global centre of excellence for training and research in bioprocessing. The skills you develop here — experimental design, laboratory technique, data analysis, troubleshooting, and scientific writing — are directly transferable to roles in biopharmaceutical R&D, process development, quality control, regulatory affairs, and academic research. Employers in this sector consistently value candidates who can demonstrate independent research competence at Master's level, including the ability to design experiments, interpret results honestly, and communicate findings to a technical audience.

## 3. Structure of the Thesis

Your thesis must follow the structure below. This is based on the School of Chemical and Bioprocess Engineering Research Project Guidelines. You may adapt section titles where appropriate, but do not omit required sections.

---

### 3.1 Title Page

The title page must include:
- Module details (module code and title)
- Project title
- Your name
- Supervisor's name
- Date of submission

---

### 3.2 Abstract (maximum 0.75 pages)

The abstract is a self-contained summary of the entire thesis. It must cover:
1. The **problem or knowledge gap** your project addressed
2. The **experimental approach** used
3. The **key findings**
4. The **significance** of those findings and any conclusions drawn

Write the abstract last — after the rest of the thesis is complete. It should be readable on its own, without reference to the main text. Do not use abbreviations in the abstract unless universally known (e.g., DNA, CHO).

---

### 3.3 Acknowledgements (maximum 0.5 pages)

A brief acknowledgement of your supervisor, any collaborators, technical staff who assisted you, and funding sources where relevant.

---

### 3.4 Nomenclature (typically no more than 1 page)

A list of abbreviations, acronyms, and symbols used in the thesis, with their definitions. Define each abbreviation on first use in the text as well.

---

### 3.5 List of Figures and List of Tables (typically no more than 1 page each)

Separate lists of all figures and tables with their numbers, titles, and page numbers. These are generated automatically in Word if you use the caption/cross-reference features.

---

### 3.6 Chapter 1 — Introduction (typically approximately 5 pages)

The introduction establishes the context and motivation for your project. It should:

1. **Establish the biological and engineering context.** What is the problem? Why does it matter in biopharmaceutical engineering? Provide the reader with enough background to understand your project.
2. **Review the relevant literature.** Summarise what is already known, citing primary peer-reviewed sources. This is not a comprehensive literature review — it should focus on work directly relevant to your specific project. Identify the gap your project addresses.
3. **State the project hypothesis or objectives clearly.** The final paragraph(s) of the introduction should state, precisely, what your project set out to test, investigate, or achieve.
4. **Outline the structure of the thesis** (optional but recommended). A brief statement of what each subsequent chapter covers helps orient the reader.

**Common mistakes:**
- An introduction that is too broad — spending several pages on general background before reaching the specific topic
- An introduction that does not culminate in a clear hypothesis or set of objectives
- Over-reliance on textbooks and review articles instead of primary research papers

---

### 3.7 Chapter 2 — Materials and Methods

This is one of the most important chapters of an experimental thesis. It must provide **sufficient detail for a competent researcher to reproduce your experiments**. Include:

- **Materials**: Reagents (with suppliers, catalogue numbers, and purity/grade where relevant), cell lines (with source and passage number), media formulations, buffers.
- **Equipment**: Key instruments (with manufacturer and model where relevant), including bioreactors, chromatography systems, analytical instruments.
- **Experimental protocols**: Step-by-step descriptions of procedures. Use past tense passive voice (e.g., "Cells were seeded at a density of…"). Include:
  - Culture conditions (temperature, CO₂, agitation rate, duration)
  - Sampling procedures and frequencies
  - Analytical methods (e.g., HPLC conditions, ELISA protocols, cell counting methods)
  - Purification steps and parameters
- **Statistical methods**: Describe how data were analysed. State the software used, the statistical tests applied, the significance thresholds, and the number of replicates.

**Important guidance on methods:**
- Do not describe the general theory behind a technique (e.g., how HPLC works). Describe what *you did* with the technique.
- If you followed a published protocol, cite it and state any modifications you made: "The protocol of [Author] (Year) was followed with the following modifications…"
- If you developed or optimised a method as part of the project, describe the optimisation process in this chapter or in a dedicated results chapter, as appropriate.

---

### 3.8 Chapter 3 onwards — Results and Discussion

You may have **one or more results chapters**, depending on the structure of your project. Each chapter should address a specific aspect of the work (e.g., "Chapter 3: Optimisation of Growth Conditions", "Chapter 4: Purification and Characterisation").

You may **combine results and discussion** within each chapter, or have separate discussion chapters. For projects with multiple results chapters, combining results and discussion within each chapter is generally easier for the reader to follow.

**Key guidance for results:**

- **Present results clearly.** Use figures, tables, and graphs to present data. Every figure and table must:
  - Be numbered sequentially (Figure 3.1, Figure 3.2, Table 3.1, etc.)
  - Have a descriptive, self-contained title/legend
  - Be referred to and discussed in the text
- **Lead with the finding, not the method.** Begin paragraphs with what you observed, not what you did.
- **Be precise.** Report numerical values, units, error bars/standard deviations, and sample sizes. Avoid vague statements ("the results improved significantly") — state by how much, with what statistical significance.
- **Report negative and inconclusive results.** If an experiment did not work, or produced an unexpected outcome, report it honestly and discuss possible reasons. This is expected and valued — it demonstrates scientific integrity and analytical thinking. Omitting failed experiments misrepresents the work.
- **Discuss optimisation steps.** If you iteratively improved a protocol or adjusted experimental conditions, present this process. Examiners want to see evidence that you understood your experimental system well enough to troubleshoot and refine it.

**Key guidance for discussion (whether integrated or separate):**

- **Interpret your results.** What do they mean? How do they relate to your hypothesis or objectives?
- **Compare with the literature.** Do your findings agree with, contradict, or extend published work? Cite specific papers and explain the comparison.
- **Acknowledge limitations.** Every experimental study has limitations (e.g., number of replicates, time constraints, equipment limitations). Identifying these honestly shows scientific maturity.
- **Do not overstate conclusions.** Your conclusions must be supported by the data you present.

---

### 3.9 Final Chapter — Conclusions and Recommendations

This chapter must:

1. **Summarise the major findings** of the project, directly addressing the hypothesis or objectives stated in the introduction. Each conclusion should be traceable to specific results presented in earlier chapters.
2. **Provide recommendations for future work.** What would the logical next steps be? What experiments would you recommend, and why? This demonstrates that you understand the broader context of your project and can think beyond the immediate findings.

Keep this chapter concise and focused. Do not introduce new data or new arguments.

---

### 3.10 Bibliography

- Use **Harvard** citation style throughout, as required by the UCD School of Chemical and Bioprocess Engineering Research Project Guidelines. See the UCD Library Harvard Style Guide for full guidance.
- Include only sources you have read and cited directly.
- All references must be traceable (include DOI or URL for online sources where available).
- Wikipedia, course notes, and non-peer-reviewed websites are **not** acceptable as references (though they may help you find primary sources).
- If you have been permitted to use a Generative AI tool and cite its output, follow the UCD Library guidance on citing AI-generated content (available via the UCD Library Harvard Style Guide).

**Example Harvard format (reference list):**
> Altschul, S.F., Gish, W., Miller, W., Myers, E.W. and Lipman, D.J. (1990) 'Basic local alignment search tool', *Journal of Molecular Biology*, 215(3), pp. 403–410. doi:10.1016/S0022-2836(05)80360-2.

---

### 3.11 Appendices

Appendices are for **supplementary material** that supports but does not drive the main argument. Typical appendix content includes:

- Raw data tables
- Sample calculations
- Extended protocol details
- Calibration curves
- Additional figures not included in the main text

**Rules for appendices:**
- Label each appendix sequentially: Appendix A, Appendix B, etc.
- Every appendix must be referred to at least once in the main text. If it is not cited, remove it.
- Appendices do not count toward the 20,000-word limit.
- Do not use appendices to circumvent the word limit by moving essential content out of the main text.

---

## 4. Figures and Tables

Figures and tables are central to an experimental thesis. They should be incorporated into the text near where they are first discussed (not collected at the end).

- **Number sequentially within each chapter** (e.g., Figure 2.1, Figure 2.2, Table 3.1).
- **Provide a descriptive title and legend.** The figure legend should allow a reader to understand the figure without reading the main text. Include: what is shown, how data were collected, what error bars represent, sample sizes.
- **Label all axes** with quantity and units.
- **Use consistent formatting** throughout the thesis.
- **Use an Equation Editor** for preparing equations (as per Research Project Guidelines).
- **Resolution**: Minimum 300 dpi for all figures. Ensure figures are legible when printed.
- **Colour**: Colour figures are acceptable; ensure they are interpretable in greyscale for accessibility.

Your submission must be your own original work. The accompanying **Student Academic Misconduct Procedure** (also effective 1 September 2024) sets out how cases are investigated and resolved. - **Generative AI tools**: There is no university-wide blanket rule permitting or prohibiting Generative AI (GenAI) tools such as ChatGPT, Copilot, or Gemini. **Permission is at the discretion of the module coordinator.** Check the module descriptor or Brightspace for a specific statement on GenAI use for this assessment. If you are unsure whether a particular use of a source or tool is acceptable, contact your supervisor **before** submitting.

## 7. Practical Advice

The following guidance is offered to help you produce a strong thesis, regardless of your prior writing experience.

**Start writing early.** Do not wait until your experiments are finished to begin writing. The Introduction, Materials and Methods, and early Results chapters can be drafted while experiments are still in progress.

**Keep a laboratory notebook throughout.** Your Methods chapter depends on accurate records. If you did not record a detail at the time, you cannot accurately report it later.

**Write for your reader.** Your reader is a technically literate engineer or scientist, but not necessarily a specialist in your precise topic. Avoid unexplained jargon. Define acronyms on first use.

**Structure your results logically.** The order in which you present results does not have to match the chronological order in which experiments were performed. Organise for clarity and narrative flow.

**Report negative results.** An experiment that did not work is not a failure of the thesis — it is a failure only if it is not reported and discussed. Examiners value honest reporting and thoughtful analysis of unexpected outcomes.

**Figures are powerful.** Invest time in clear, well-labelled, high-quality figures. A well-designed figure communicates data more efficiently than a paragraph of text.

**Use the literature actively.** The strongest theses do not just cite a paper — they explain what it found and how that relates to your work.

**Seek feedback.** Share draft chapters with your supervisor before the deadline. Their feedback is part of the learning process.

**Proofread carefully.** Read the final draft aloud. Spelling and grammatical errors in an otherwise strong thesis are avoidable and reduce the overall impression.
