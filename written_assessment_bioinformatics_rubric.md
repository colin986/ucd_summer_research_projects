# CHEN40140 Biopharmaceutical Engineering Project
## MEngSc in Biopharmaceutical Engineering — Summer Research Project
## Written Assessment: Grading Rubric
### Short Research Communication (Letter to the Editor Style)
### Bioinformatics Projects Only

---

> **Scope:** This rubric applies exclusively to **bioinformatics summer research projects**. All projects are entirely computational — no wet laboratory or experimental work is performed by students. Examiners should assess Methods sections for the rigour of the computational pipeline (databases, tools, parameters) and Results sections for the quality of bioinformatics outputs (e.g., sequence alignments, phylogenetic analyses, structural predictions, expression analyses, pathway analyses). References to experimental validation are appropriate only where students are discussing published experimental work from the literature, not their own.

The rubric is divided into **four assessed components**, each weighted as shown. Within each component, five performance levels are defined — corresponding to the five broad grade bands above. The per-component mark ranges are calibrated so that a student consistently scoring at the bottom of a given band will receive a total mark that falls within the corresponding grade range on the Alternative Linear Conversion Scale.

## Component A: Scientific Content and Critical Thinking (50 marks)

*This component assesses whether the student understands their own project: the question they asked, what they did, what they found, and what it means. It is the most heavily weighted component because the ability to conduct and communicate rigorous bioinformatics research is the primary learning outcome of this project.*

*Sub-components: Research question and objectives (10 marks); Methods (10 marks); Results (15 marks); Discussion and conclusions (15 marks).*

---

### A1. Research Question and Objectives (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | The research question is precisely and elegantly framed. The biological/engineering significance is convincingly established. The specific aim of the study is unambiguous and logically motivated by the preceding context. The reader is left in no doubt about what the study set out to achieve and why it matters. |
| **B** | 7–8 | The research question is clearly stated and well-motivated. The significance of the work is established with good use of context. The specific aim is identifiable and appropriate. Minor improvements in precision or framing are possible but the overall rationale is sound. |
| **C** | 6 | A research question is present and broadly understandable. The rationale is given but may be somewhat generic or not fully specific to the study at hand. The aim may be stated in general terms rather than precisely scoped. The reader understands what was done but the "why" is not fully convincing. |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | A research question is implied but not clearly articulated. The significance of the work is described in very general terms with little specificity. The aim of the study may be confused with the methods or results. Considerable improvement needed for this to meet the standard required. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | No clear research question is identifiable. The introduction does not establish why the work was done or what it sought to achieve. Fundamental misunderstanding of the project's purpose is evident, or the section is absent. |

---

### A2. Methods (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | The methods are described with precision and completeness. All databases, tools, and key parameters are correctly identified with versions and access dates where relevant. The analytical pipeline is presented in a logical sequence. A competent reader could reproduce the analysis from this description. Choices of tools or approaches are implicitly or explicitly justified. |
| **B** | 7–8 | The methods are largely complete and accurate. Key tools and databases are identified. Most parameters and analytical steps are described. Minor omissions (e.g., a tool version, an access date) do not undermine reproducibility. The pipeline is presented in a broadly logical order. |
| **C** | 6 | The methods describe what was done at a general level. Major tools and databases are mentioned but details (versions, parameters, specific steps) are incomplete. A reader could understand the general approach taken but could not reproduce the analysis without additional information. Some logical gaps in the pipeline description. |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | The methods are superficial or disorganised. Important tools or databases may be missing. The description reads more like a narrative of what the student did than a reproducible protocol. Key decisions (e.g., why a particular tool was chosen) are absent. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | The methods section is absent, incoherent, or so incomplete as to be scientifically meaningless. It is not possible to determine from this section what analyses were performed. |

---

### A3. Results (15 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 13–15 | Results are presented with clarity, precision, and appropriate scientific rigour. Findings are led by the result itself, not by the procedure. All figures and tables are referenced in text and have informative, self-contained legends. Data are quantified where possible. The structure of the Results section builds logically toward the conclusions. Figures are of high quality and efficiently communicate the data. |
| **B** | 11–12 | Results are clearly presented and the key findings are identifiable. Most figures and tables are well-integrated and appropriately labelled. Some findings could be quantified or described with greater precision, but the overall presentation is sound. The logical flow is mostly clear with minor gaps. |
| **C** | 8–10 | Results are present and the main findings can be understood. Figures and tables may have minor deficiencies (e.g., incomplete legends, inconsistent labelling). Some findings are described in vague terms. The section may contain some interpretation that belongs in the Discussion, or vice versa. The overall picture is understandable but lacks precision. |
| **D / D-** | 6–7 | Results are incomplete, disorganised, or difficult to follow. Key findings may be missing or described so vaguely as to be uninformative. Figures and tables may be absent, unlabelled, or not referred to in the text. The distinction between results and discussion is blurred throughout. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | Results are absent, incoherent, or consist largely of unprocessed outputs from software with no narrative. There is no meaningful communication of what was found. |

---

### A4. Discussion and Conclusions (15 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 13–15 | The discussion demonstrates genuine critical thinking. Key findings are interpreted confidently and correctly in the context of the research question. Literature is actively engaged: the student explains how their findings compare to, extend, or challenge prior work, rather than merely citing it. Limitations are identified with specificity and intellectual honesty. Conclusions are precise, directly answer the stated research question, and are fully supported by the results presented. The student demonstrates scientific maturity. |
| **B** | 11–12 | Key findings are interpreted accurately and contextualised with relevant literature. The discussion is logically structured and demonstrates a good understanding of the data. Limitations are acknowledged in a meaningful way. Conclusions are clear and supported by the results. Some scope for deeper critical engagement or more nuanced interpretation. |
| **C** | 8–10 | The discussion interprets the main findings but may be somewhat descriptive or superficial. Literature references are included but engagement is limited (papers cited without explanation of their relevance). Limitations are acknowledged at a general level. Conclusions are present but may be overstated, understated, or not clearly linked back to the original research question. |
| **D / D-** | 6–7 | The discussion largely restates the results without interpretation. Critical engagement with the literature is minimal or absent. Limitations are not addressed or are dismissed. Conclusions are vague, unsupported, or contradict the results. Evidence of significant misunderstanding of the data or its meaning. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | The discussion is absent, incoherent, or cannot be distinguished from the Results. There is no meaningful interpretation of the findings. Conclusions are absent or fundamentally incorrect. |

## Component B: Structure and Adherence to Format (15 marks)

*This component assesses whether the student has followed the prescribed format of a short research communication and whether the document is logically organised. In professional practice, adherence to a publication format is a non-negotiable requirement.*

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 13–15 | All required sections are present, correctly ordered, and appropriately proportioned. The abstract is within the word limit and covers all four required elements (context, approach, finding, significance). Word count is within the specified range. Title is informative and precise. Keywords are appropriate and well-chosen. The document reads as a coherent, professionally structured research communication. |
| **B** | 11–12 | All required sections are present and correctly ordered. The abstract is mostly effective; it may miss one element or be slightly over/under length. Word count is within or close to the specified range. Minor issues with proportionality between sections do not detract significantly from the overall structure. |
| **C** | 8–10 | All major sections are present but one or more may be poorly proportioned, too brief, or too long relative to the format. The abstract may have significant omissions. Word count may be outside the range. The document is recognisable as a research communication but structural weaknesses are apparent. |
| **D / D-** | 6–7 | One or more required sections are missing or so minimal as to be ineffective. The structure deviates substantially from the prescribed format. The document reads more like a report or essay than a research communication. Word count may be significantly outside the prescribed range. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | The submission does not follow the prescribed format. Sections are absent, scrambled, or unlabelled. The document does not resemble a research communication. |

## Component C: Written Communication (20 marks)

*This component assesses the clarity, concision, and professional quality of the student's writing. It explicitly recognises that students write in English as a second or third language. The standard assessed is appropriate academic scientific writing — not native-speaker fluency. Grammatical errors that do not impede understanding are penalised less heavily than structural or clarity issues.*

*Sub-components: Clarity and precision of expression (10 marks); Grammar, spelling, and academic register (10 marks).*

---

### C1. Clarity and Precision of Expression (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | Writing is consistently clear, precise, and well-constructed. Technical concepts are explained at the appropriate level for the audience. Paragraphs are cohesive and transitions between ideas are logical. The student writes with confidence and authority. Sentences are appropriately concise — information density is high without being difficult to parse. |
| **B** | 7–8 | Writing is mostly clear and precise. Occasional sentences are ambiguous or unnecessarily complex, but these do not significantly impede understanding. The overall argument is easy to follow. The student demonstrates good control of technical language. |
| **C** | 6 | The writing is generally understandable but clarity is inconsistent. Some passages are vague, overly complex, or poorly structured. Technical terms may be used imprecisely. The overall meaning can be extracted with effort. |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | Significant clarity issues throughout. Many passages are difficult to understand. Vague or imprecise language is frequent. The reader must work hard to extract meaning. Technical concepts may be substantially misrepresented in the writing. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | The writing is largely incomprehensible or so poorly structured that the content cannot be assessed. |

---

### C2. Grammar, Spelling, and Academic Register (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | Writing is essentially free from grammatical and spelling errors. Academic register is consistently maintained (e.g., appropriate use of passive voice in Methods; avoidance of informal language). Tense usage is consistent and correct. Abbreviations are defined on first use. |
| **B** | 7–8 | Writing has occasional grammatical or spelling errors that do not impede understanding. Academic register is mostly maintained with minor lapses. Tense usage is mostly consistent. Overall impression is professional. |
| **C** | 6 | Grammatical and/or spelling errors are present but do not prevent the text from being understood. Some informal phrasing or inconsistent register. Errors are more frequent than would be expected in a professional submission but do not obscure meaning. *Note: Students writing in English as an additional language who demonstrate good control of scientific content will not be penalised for occasional grammatical constructions consistent with second-language writing, provided meaning is clear.* |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | Frequent grammatical and spelling errors that impede reading. Register is frequently inappropriate. Errors are sufficiently numerous or severe to distract from the scientific content. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | The text is not sufficiently grammatically coherent to be assessed as scientific writing. Errors are pervasive and prevent understanding. |

## Component D: Engagement with the Literature (15 marks)

*This component assesses the quality and appropriate use of references. In research, engaging critically with published work is a core professional competency.*

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 13–15 | References are high quality (peer-reviewed primary literature is predominant), directly relevant to the specific points they support, and correctly formatted in Harvard style. The literature is actively engaged in the Discussion: the student explains what cited papers found and how this relates to their own work. There is no over-reliance on any single source. The reference list demonstrates that the student has read broadly and selected sources judiciously. |
| **B** | 11–12 | References are mostly from primary peer-reviewed literature and are relevant and correctly formatted, with minor errors. Literature is engaged meaningfully in the Discussion, though depth of engagement may vary. The reference list shows good but not exceptional breadth and selectivity. |
| **C** | 8–10 | References are present and are mostly relevant. Some reliance on secondary sources (reviews, textbooks) rather than primary literature. Formatting has consistent errors. In-text citations are used but the Discussion may cite papers without explaining their content or relevance. The reference list is adequate but limited in scope or quality. |
| **D / D-** | 6–7 | References are limited in number, relevance, or quality. Significant use of non-peer-reviewed sources (websites, Wikipedia). Formatting is inconsistent or incorrect throughout. Citations in the text are sparse or used inappropriately. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | References are largely absent, non-existent, or entirely inappropriate (e.g., only websites or textbooks cited). No meaningful engagement with the scientific literature is evident. |

## Guidance Notes for Examiners

- **Word count penalties:** Submissions exceeding 2,750 words (10% above maximum) should have 5 marks deducted from Component B before scoring. Submissions below 1,800 words should be noted and Component A scored accordingly.
- **Academic integrity:** Where Turnitin or equivalent flags concerns, refer to the UCD Academic Integrity Policy before completing marking. Do not award marks to content that appears to have been generated by AI or copied without attribution.
- **Language equity:** Component C2 descriptors explicitly accommodate second-language writers. Grammatical patterns that are consistent with a specific first-language background should not be penalised if meaning is clear. This does not apply to vagueness or imprecision (assessed under C1).
- **Second examiner review:** All submissions awarded a grade of D/D- or below, or A/A+, should be reviewed by a second examiner before grades are released, in accordance with UCD assessment regulations.
