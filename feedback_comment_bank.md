# Feedback Comment Bank
## MEngSc Biopharmaceutical Engineering — Written Assessment

**How to use this document:**
Find the component and the performance level that matches the submission. Copy the relevant phrase(s), paste into the feedback field of the grading tracker, and personalise the bracketed placeholders. You do not need to use every phrase — select 2–4 that are most relevant to the individual submission. Always include at least one strength and one area for improvement per student.

---

## COMPONENT A1 — Research Question & Objectives

### Distinction (9–10)
- "The research question is framed with precision and clarity. The biological significance of [topic] is convincingly established, and the specific aim of the study is unambiguous from the outset."
- "The introduction builds a compelling rationale for investigating [topic], leading logically and efficiently to a well-scoped research question."
- "An excellent introduction that establishes both the 'what' and the 'why' of the study with confidence. The specific aim is stated with the kind of precision expected in a published short communication."

### Merit (7–8)
- "The research question is clearly communicated and the rationale is sound. [Minor issue, e.g.: the final aim statement could be tightened to be more specific about the scope of the analysis.]"
- "Good contextualisation of the research problem. The significance of [topic] is established, though the specific aim would benefit from greater precision — the reader is clear on the topic but slightly less certain about the exact question being answered."
- "A well-motivated introduction. Consider that the last sentence of the introduction should state your aim with enough specificity that the reader knows exactly what the analysis will test or determine."

### Pass (5–6)
- "A research question is present and understandable, but it is stated in general terms rather than as a focused, testable aim. The introduction provides background but does not lead the reader directly to the specific analysis performed."
- "The context for [topic] is established, but the significance is described generically rather than specifically to your project. Strengthening the final aim statement — making it more specific — would substantially improve this section."
- "The introduction covers the relevant background but spends [too much space on general context / not enough space on the specific gap] relative to the aim of this particular study."

### Marginal Fail (3–4)
- "A research question is implied but not clearly articulated. The reader must infer the aim of the study from the methods rather than from the introduction. In a research communication, the aim must be explicitly stated."
- "The introduction describes [topic] in general terms but does not establish a specific knowledge gap that this project addresses. The connection between the background and the specific analysis is not made clear."
- "The specific aim of this study is unclear from the introduction. This is a significant weakness because the reader cannot evaluate whether the results answer the question the study set out to address."

### Fail (0–2)
- "No discernible research question or specific aim can be identified. The introduction does not establish why this work was conducted or what it sought to achieve. This is a fundamental requirement of a research communication."
- "The introduction does not identify a knowledge gap or state a research aim. Considerable development is needed in this section."

## COMPONENT A2 — Methods

### Distinction (9–10)
- "The methods section is detailed, well-structured, and reproducible. All key tools are named with versions [e.g. IQ-TREE 2.1.4], databases are identified with accession information and access dates, and the analytical pipeline is presented in a logical sequence."
- "An excellent methods section that gives the reader everything needed to understand and reproduce the analysis. The choice of [tool/approach] is appropriate and the parameters used are clearly stated."
- "The pipeline is described with precision and in the correct logical order. This section exemplifies the standard expected in a published bioinformatics study."

### Merit (7–8)
- "The methods are largely complete and the overall approach is clear. [Minor omission, e.g.: tool versions are not consistently reported / the E-value threshold used in the BLAST search is not stated.] These are minor gaps that do not undermine the overall approach."
- "Key tools and databases are correctly identified. The analytical pipeline is logical. One improvement: [specific gap, e.g.: the criteria used to select sequences for downstream analysis are not described], which would be needed for full reproducibility."

### Pass (5–6)
- "The methods identify the key tools used but lack the detail required for reproducibility. [Specific gap, e.g.: no parameters are reported for the sequence alignment step; the version of the tool is not stated; the database search strategy is not described.] A reader could understand the general approach but could not reproduce the analysis."
- "The description of the pipeline is at a general level. Key details — particularly [parameters / thresholds / version numbers / selection criteria] — are missing. These details are not optional in a methods section; they are required for scientific transparency."
- "The methods section reads more as a narrative account of activities than as a reproducible protocol. Restructure this section to follow the logical sequence of the analysis rather than the order in which you personally encountered the steps."

### Marginal Fail (3–4)
- "The methods section is too brief and too general to convey how the analysis was conducted. [Key tool/database] is not mentioned. The reader cannot determine the scope or rigour of the analytical pipeline from this section."
- "Significant gaps in the methods section mean that the analysis cannot be evaluated for rigour or reproduced. This section requires substantial expansion in future work."

### Fail (0–2)
- "The methods section is absent / so incomplete that it is not possible to determine what analyses were performed. This is a critical omission in a research communication."

## COMPONENT A3 — Results

### Distinction (13–15)
- "Results are presented with clarity and precision. The narrative leads with findings rather than methods [e.g.: 'Phylogenetic analysis revealed two distinct clades' rather than 'Phylogenetic analysis was performed and the tree is shown in Figure 1'], and the figures are well-designed and self-contained."
- "The selection of results is judicious — the most important findings are presented in the main text, supported by appropriately labelled figures, and supplementary material is used effectively for the complete dataset."
- "An excellent results section. The logical structure builds convincingly toward the discussion, and each finding is stated with the precision expected in primary research literature."
- "Figure [X] is particularly effective — the [labelling / colour scheme / layout] communicates the [finding] efficiently."

### Merit (10–12)
- "Key results are clearly communicated and supported by appropriate figures. [Minor issue, e.g.: Figure 2 would benefit from clearer axis labels / the percentage identity values cited in the text should be stated more precisely / the supplementary table should be referenced earlier in the text.]"
- "The main findings are identifiable and mostly presented with precision. One area for improvement: [e.g.: some passages describe what was done rather than what was found — try to lead each paragraph with the result itself.]"

### Pass (7–9)
- "The main findings can be understood, but the results section relies too heavily on describing procedures rather than reporting findings. Restructuring paragraphs to open with the result and then reference the supporting figure would substantially improve this section."
- "Figure [X] is not referenced in the text / lacks a self-contained legend. Every figure must be explicitly cited in the results section and the legend must allow a reader to understand the figure without reading the main text."
- "Some findings are described in vague terms (e.g., 'the results showed differences between the sequences'). Scientific writing requires precision — state what the difference was and how large it was."
- "There is a blurring of results and interpretation in this section. Reserve statements about what the results mean for the Discussion."
- "The complete gene list in [Supplementary Table S1 / the main text] is not effectively summarised in the narrative. The reader needs to understand the key patterns from the results text; the table provides the detail but should not replace a narrative summary."

### Marginal Fail (4–6)
- "Results are incomplete — [key finding/analysis] is not reported in this section despite being discussed later. The results section must present all findings that are referred to in the Discussion."
- "Figures are present but are not referenced in the text / have insufficient labels / are too small to interpret. Figures that cannot be read do not contribute to the assessment."
- "The results section consists largely of descriptions of what was done rather than what was found. This section needs to be substantially rewritten to report the outputs of the analyses rather than the process."

### Fail (0–3)
- "The results section does not communicate what was found. [It largely repeats the methods / it consists of raw software output without narrative / key findings are absent.] This section requires complete redrafting."

## COMPONENT A4 — Discussion & Conclusions

### Distinction (13–15)
- "An impressive discussion that demonstrates genuine critical thinking. The key findings are interpreted confidently and placed in meaningful context by reference to [specific papers]. The student clearly understands what their data does and does not show."
- "The limitation identified — [specific limitation, e.g.: the reliance on reference genome availability in NCBI, which may under-represent non-model organisms] — is specific and shows scientific maturity. This level of honest reflection distinguishes strong scientific writing."
- "The conclusions are precise, directly answer the research question stated in the introduction, and are fully supported by the results presented. The take-home message is clear and appropriately scoped."
- "The engagement with the literature is active rather than passive — rather than simply citing papers, the student explains what [Author et al.] found and how this compares to or extends the findings of the current study."

### Merit (10–12)
- "Key findings are interpreted accurately and the significance is explained clearly. The discussion would be strengthened by [deeper engagement with one or two specific papers / a more specific limitation / a more direct link between the conclusions and the original research question]."
- "The conclusions are sound and appropriately supported by the results. One improvement: the final conclusion statement should directly revisit the specific aim stated in the introduction, to close the scientific loop explicitly."
- "The literature is cited appropriately but in a somewhat passive way — papers are referenced but not always explained. Stronger academic writing will note *what the cited study found* and explain whether your results support, contradict, or extend that finding."

### Pass (7–9)
- "The discussion is present but largely descriptive — it tends to restate results rather than interpret them. The key question to ask when writing a discussion is: 'So what does this mean?' rather than 'What did I find?'"
- "Limitations are acknowledged only in generic terms [e.g.: 'the study is limited by the size of the dataset']. A stronger limitation identifies the *specific consequence* of that limitation for your particular findings and conclusions."
- "The conclusions are present but would be strengthened by directly revisiting the research question stated in the introduction. The reader should be able to map each conclusion back to the aim."
- "The discussion cites relevant literature but does not explain what those studies found. Simply citing a reference is not sufficient — the citation must be accompanied by a brief explanation of its relevance."

### Marginal Fail (4–6)
- "The discussion predominantly restates the results without providing interpretation. Scientific interpretation requires the student to explain what the results mean in the context of [the research question / the broader field / existing knowledge]."
- "The conclusions are vague / overstated / not supported by the results presented. Conclusions must be directly evidenced by the results in the main text — they cannot go beyond what the data shows."
- "No limitations are identified. Every computational study has limitations; acknowledging them is not a weakness but a demonstration of scientific integrity."
- "There is minimal or no engagement with the published literature in the discussion. This is a significant weakness — the discussion must place your findings in the context of what is already known."

### Fail (0–3)
- "The discussion section is absent / cannot be distinguished from the results section / does not contain any meaningful interpretation of the findings."
- "The conclusions contradict the results presented, or are not supported by any data in the submission."

## COMPONENT B — Structure & Format

### Distinction (13–15)
- "The submission adheres closely to the prescribed format. All sections are present, well-proportioned, and logically ordered. The abstract effectively summarises the context, approach, key finding, and significance within the word limit."
- "The title is precise and informative — it communicates the topic, approach, and finding at a glance. Keywords are well-chosen."
- "This reads as a polished research communication. The structure supports the argument effectively."

### Merit (10–12)
- "All required sections are present and well-ordered. [Minor issue, e.g.: the abstract does not clearly state the significance of the finding / the keyword list would benefit from more specific terms / the introduction is slightly over-proportioned relative to the results.]"

### Pass (7–9)
- "The overall structure is present but [one or more sections are notably over- or under-proportioned / the abstract is missing one of its four required elements / the word count is outside the specified range]."
- "The abstract [exceeds 200 words / does not clearly state the approach used / does not state the significance of the findings]. The abstract is the most-read section of any research communication and should receive careful attention."
- "Supplementary material is present but [one or more items are not cited in the main text / legends are missing / items are not clearly numbered]. Each supplementary item must be cited in the main text and have a descriptive legend."

### Marginal Fail (4–6)
- "The submission is missing [section]. This is a required component of the prescribed format."
- "The document does not follow the prescribed format — it reads more as [a laboratory report / an essay] than a short research communication. The structure should follow: Abstract → Keywords → Introduction → Methods → Results → Discussion → References."
- "The word count is significantly [above/below] the specified range. This affects the score for this component."

### Fail (0–3)
- "The submission does not follow the prescribed format. Required sections are [absent / in an incorrect order]. This is a fundamental requirement of the assessment."

## COMPONENT C — Written Communication

### Distinction (C, 18–20 combined)
- "Writing is consistently clear, precise, and professional. The student demonstrates excellent command of scientific English and writes with confidence and authority appropriate to the subject matter."
- "The Methods section is written in the correct passive past tense throughout; the Discussion maintains appropriate academic register. Technical terminology is used precisely and accurately."

### Merit (C, 14–17 combined)
- "Writing is mostly clear and professional with occasional lapses. [e.g.: a few sentences in the Results section are ambiguous and could be rephrased for clarity / informal phrasing appears occasionally in the Discussion]."
- "Good overall writing quality. The student demonstrates solid control of scientific language with minor inconsistencies in tense or register."

### Pass (C, 10–13 combined)
- "The writing is generally understandable but clarity is inconsistent. [e.g.: several sentences in the Discussion section are difficult to parse on first reading / technical terms are occasionally used imprecisely / some paragraphs contain multiple ideas that would benefit from being separated]."
- "There are grammatical and spelling errors throughout, but they do not prevent the scientific content from being understood. Careful proofreading before submission would reduce these significantly."
- *(If second-language writer):* "The scientific content is communicated clearly despite occasional grammatical constructions that reflect second-language writing. The student demonstrates good command of technical vocabulary and scientific structure."

### Marginal Fail (C, 6–9 combined)
- "Clarity issues are significant enough to interfere with communicating the science. [e.g.: key results are described in language too vague to be informative / several passages in the Discussion require multiple readings to understand]."
- "Grammatical errors are frequent enough to distract from the content. The submission would benefit from thorough proofreading, and the student should consider using UCD's academic writing support services."

### Fail (C, 0–5 combined)
- "The writing is not sufficiently coherent to be assessed as scientific writing. The science cannot be evaluated independently of the language because the language prevents understanding."

## COMPONENT D — Literature Engagement

### Distinction (13–15)
- "References are high quality, directly relevant, and correctly formatted in Vancouver style. The literature is actively engaged in the Discussion — the student explains what cited papers found and how this compares to the present study. The breadth and selectivity of the reference list demonstrates genuine engagement with the field."
- "[Author et al., year] is particularly well integrated — the student clearly explains the relevant finding and draws a direct comparison with their own results."

### Merit (10–12)
- "The reference list is mostly composed of primary peer-reviewed literature and is directly relevant. [Minor issue, e.g.: two or three references have formatting inconsistencies / literature is cited in the Discussion but the content of the cited papers is not always explained]."
- "Good literature engagement overall. To strengthen this further, aim to explain what each cited study found rather than simply citing it — this shows the reader that you understand the literature, not just that you have read it."

### Pass (7–9)
- "The reference list includes some relevant primary literature but relies more heavily than ideal on [reviews / textbooks / websites]. Primary research articles should form the majority of your reference list."
- "References are cited in the text but the Discussion does not explain the content of the cited papers. Simply citing a reference with no context (e.g., 'thermostability is important in biopharmaceutical applications [5]') is the minimum standard — explain *what* reference [5] found."
- "Vancouver formatting is inconsistent throughout [e.g.: some references are missing journal volume/issue numbers / DOIs are absent / author initials are formatted differently between references]."

### Marginal Fail (4–6)
- "The reference list contains significant quality issues: [heavy use of websites and non-peer-reviewed sources / very few references for a study of this scope / references are cited but not relevant to the claims they are meant to support]."
- "The Discussion contains claims that are not supported by citations. All factual claims about the published literature must be attributed to a specific source."

### Fail (0–3)
- "The reference list is largely absent or consists entirely of inappropriate sources [websites, Wikipedia, unattributed material]. There is no meaningful engagement with the peer-reviewed scientific literature."

## General Feedback Phrases

**Opening positive statements (use for all passing submissions):**
- "This is a competent submission that demonstrates a solid understanding of [topic] and the bioinformatics tools used."
- "The student has engaged seriously with this project and the submission reflects genuine effort to communicate the research clearly."
- "A strong submission overall, with particularly effective [identification of section, e.g.: results presentation / literature engagement / discussion of limitations]."

**Closing developmental statements:**
- "The primary area for development is [area]. Addressing this in future scientific writing will substantially strengthen the quality of your communications."
- "Overall, this submission demonstrates [competency] and the student is encouraged to [specific action, e.g.: read more primary literature in this area / practise writing results-led paragraphs / seek feedback on drafts before submitting]."
- "This submission shows real promise. The scientific thinking is sound; the main opportunity for development is in the precision and structure of the written communication."
- "I encourage the student to read a published Letter to the Editor in *Biotechnology and Bioengineering* and compare the discussion section structure to their own — this will clarify the standard expected."

**Supplementary-specific feedback:**
- "The supplementary material is well-used: the full gene list in Supplementary Table S1 is cited in the main text and has clear column headers, allowing the reader to verify the results presented in Figure 2."
- "Supplementary Table S1 contains a complete list of genes but lacks clear column headers and is not cited in the main text at the relevant point. Every supplementary item must be explicitly referenced in the main text."
- "Several supplementary figures are not cited anywhere in the main text. Uncited supplementary material should be removed — if it is not important enough to mention in the main text, it should not be included."
- "The decision to keep the phylogenetic tree in the main text and place the full alignment in Supplementary is sound and reflects good editorial judgement."
