## 3. How to Record Your Presentation in PowerPoint

The steps below are for Microsoft PowerPoint (Windows and Mac versions are similar). If you use a different version, UCD's IT Services website has further guidance.

1. Open your presentation in PowerPoint.
2. Go to the **Slideshow** tab → click **Record Slide Show** (Windows) or **Record** (Mac).
3. Choose **Record from Beginning**.
4. Advance through your slides naturally, speaking your narration as you go. Your timings are recorded automatically.
5. When finished, stop the recording.
6. **Save As** → select **PowerPoint Presentation (.pptx)**.
7. Confirm the file size is larger than before recording — this confirms the audio is embedded.
8. Open the file on a different device to verify the audio plays correctly before submitting.

If you make an error on a slide, you can re-record individual slides: right-click the slide in the panel → **Record Slide**.

## 4. Suggested Slide Structure

You have approximately 5 minutes. Plan your narration before you record — do not read from slides. The following structure is a guide; you may adapt it if there is a compelling reason to do so, but do not deviate substantially.

---

### Slide 1: Title Slide (~20 seconds)
- Presentation title
- Your name and student number
- Supervisor's name
- Date
- *Narrate:* Briefly introduce yourself, the title of your project, and your supervisor. Keep this very short.

---

### Slide 2: Background and Research Question (~60 seconds)
- 3–5 bullet points or a brief visual establishing the context
- End with a clear statement of your research question or aim

*Narrate:* Explain why this problem matters in biopharmaceutical engineering. What is the knowledge gap? What did you set out to find? By the end of this slide, the examiner should know exactly what question your project is trying to answer.

**Common mistake:** Spending too long on background. Your examiner is a specialist. Provide only the context your findings cannot be understood without.

---

### Slide 3: Methods (~45 seconds)
- A simple schematic or pipeline diagram is strongly preferred over a list of bullet points
- Name the key tools and databases; do not detail every step

*Narrate:* Give the examiner enough to understand *how* you answered your question. Focus on the approach, not the details. A visual pipeline (e.g., flowchart: data source → tool → output) communicates a bioinformatics workflow more efficiently than text.

---

### Slides 4–5: Results (~90–100 seconds total)
- One key finding per slide
- Every slide should contain a figure, table, or visual output — not bullet points of text
- Use slide titles as "take-home" headlines (e.g., "Clade A enzymes show significantly higher predicted thermostability")

*Narrate:* Explain what the figure shows and what it means. State the result clearly and precisely. Do not describe what you did — describe what you found.

**Prioritise.** In 5 minutes you cannot present all your findings. Choose the two or three most important results and present those well, rather than presenting everything superficially.

---

### Slide 6: Discussion and Conclusions (~55 seconds)
- 3–4 bullets covering: what this means; how it relates to existing work; key limitation; take-home message

*Narrate:* Interpret your key finding. Why does it matter? What does it add to what was already known? Be honest about one significant limitation of your analysis. End with a single, memorable take-home message.

---

### Slide 7 (optional): Future Directions or Acknowledgements (~15 seconds)
- Brief note on logical next steps, or acknowledgement of supervisor and any data sources

## 5. Timing

**Five minutes is a strict limit.** Examiners will note if the presentation is significantly over or under time, and this will affect your score under the Delivery component (see Rubric).

Recommendations:
- Rehearse at least three times before recording. Experienced presenters rehearse more.
- Record yourself once and listen back. Are you speaking clearly? Are you rushing?
- Write a brief script or detailed notes before your first rehearsal. You do not need to memorise it word-for-word, but knowing what you want to say on each slide prevents you from rushing or losing your train of thought.

Target approximate slide timings are given in Section 4. You will find that some slides take slightly longer or shorter — this is normal. What matters is that your total narration is close to 5 minutes.

## 6. Slide Design Principles

Good slides support the speaker — they do not replace the speaker.

**Do:**
- Use a clean, professional template with consistent fonts and colours
- Use figures, diagrams, and visuals wherever possible — this is especially important for bioinformatics results (phylogenetic trees, alignments, heatmaps, structure visualisations)
- Use large, readable fonts (minimum 24pt for body text)
- Label all axes, figures, and tables clearly
- Ensure sufficient contrast between text and background
- Use slide titles as "headline" statements rather than generic labels (e.g., "Results" is weak; "Phylogenetic analysis reveals two distinct clades" is strong)

**Do not:**
- Paste large blocks of text onto slides
- Include full Methods tables or reference lists
- Use more than 2–3 colours in your colour scheme
- Use animations that distract from the content
- Paste terminal output or raw code output directly onto slides — summarise or visualise the result instead

**Accessibility:** If you use colour to distinguish data (e.g., in a phylogenetic tree or heatmap), ensure your colour choices are distinguishable for colour-blind viewers. Tools such as ColorBrewer or the Viz Palette website can help you choose accessible colour palettes.

## 7. Delivery Guidance

You do not need to be a professional public speaker to score well in this assessment. You do need to:

- **Speak clearly.** This is particularly important for international students. You do not need to change your accent — speak at a pace that a listener unfamiliar with your background can follow. Clarity of pronunciation of key technical terms matters more than general fluency.
- **Speak from notes, not from slides.** Your slides should be visual aids, not a script. If you are reading directly from the slide, you are not presenting — you are narrating text.
- **Speak to the listener.** Imagine you are explaining your work to an interested colleague, not reading a report aloud.
- **Vary your pace.** Slow down for important findings. Speak slightly faster through the methods.
- **Avoid filler words.** Frequent "um", "uh", "you know" can distract the listener. Silence is better than a filler word — it gives the listener time to absorb information.

If English is not your first language: write your narration script in full before recording. Practise it until it feels natural. You will not be penalised for an accent, and minor grammatical errors in speech are expected and accommodated in grading. Clear, accurate communication of scientific content is the priority.

## 8. Academic Integrity

The presentation must represent your own work and your own words. UCD's **Academic Integrity Policy** (effective 1 September 2024) and the accompanying **Student Academic Misconduct Procedure** apply to this assessment in the same way as to the written submission.

- The scientific content of the presentation must be based on the bioinformatics work you performed. You may not present results you did not generate or misrepresent the scope of your analysis.
- **Generative AI tools**: Permission to use GenAI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Copilot) is at the discretion of the module coordinator. Check the module descriptor or Brightspace for a specific statement on GenAI use for this assessment, and consult your supervisor if unclear. Where use is permitted, it must be acknowledged explicitly. Using AI to generate the narration script you record, without permission and acknowledgement, constitutes academic misconduct under the 2024 policy.
- Fabrication of results or misrepresentation of your bioinformatics analysis in the presentation is research misconduct.
- **Late submission**: The same late submission penalties apply as for the written component — one grade point reduction per 5 working days late, work not accepted after 10 working days without prior Extenuating Circumstances approval.

## 9. Technical Troubleshooting

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Audio is not embedded | When saving, choose "Embed audio" or re-record and save as .pptx. Do not save as .ppsx only. |
| File is too large to upload | Compress your images before recording (PowerPoint: File → Compress Media). |
| Audio quality is poor | Use a headset or external microphone if possible. Record in a quiet room. |
| Timing is too short/long | Re-record individual slides. Do not artificially pad or rush — revise your content. |
| Cannot access PowerPoint | UCD provides Office 365 to all registered students free of charge via the UCD IT Services portal. |

If you encounter technical difficulties that you cannot resolve before the deadline, contact your supervisor immediately — do not wait until after the deadline.
