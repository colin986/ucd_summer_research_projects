The rubric is divided into **four assessed components**, each weighted as shown. Within each component, five performance levels are defined — corresponding to the five broad grade bands above. The per-component mark ranges are calibrated so that a student consistently scoring at the bottom of a given band will receive a total mark that falls within the corresponding grade range on the Alternative Linear Conversion Scale. Examiners should assign a mark within the appropriate band rather than defaulting to the midpoint; use descriptors to calibrate within bands.

## Component A: Scientific Content (50 marks)

*This component assesses whether the student understands and can communicate the research: the question, the approach, the findings, and their significance. It is the most heavily weighted component. The examiner should assess content independently of presentation quality — a student who communicates weak science fluently should not score highly on this component, and a student who communicates strong science less fluently should not be unduly penalised here.*

*Sub-components: Research question and context (10 marks); Methods (10 marks); Results (15 marks); Discussion and conclusions (15 marks).*

---

### A1. Research Question and Context (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | The biological/engineering problem is established with confidence and precision. The significance of the research question is made immediately clear. By the end of the Background/Introduction slide(s), the examiner knows exactly what the project set out to answer and why it matters. Context is specific to the project, not generic. |
| **B** | 7–8 | The research question is clearly communicated and the context is well-established. The significance is explained convincingly, though minor imprecision or excess background may be present. The examiner is not left in doubt about what the project is trying to achieve. |
| **C** | 6 | A research question is communicated but may lack precision or be stated only in general terms. The context is provided but may be too broad or may not lead the listener logically to the specific aim. The examiner can identify the project's purpose but may have to infer it. |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | The research question is unclear or implicit. Context is provided but is either too generic or does not connect to the specific project. The examiner is uncertain about what the project set out to achieve. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | No discernible research question. Background is either absent or entirely disconnected from the project. The purpose of the work cannot be understood from the presentation. |

---

### A2. Methods (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | The bioinformatics approach is communicated clearly and at an appropriate level of detail for a 5-minute presentation. Key tools, databases, and the logic of the analytical pipeline are conveyed efficiently, ideally supported by a visual schematic. The examiner understands *how* the research question was addressed without being overwhelmed by technical minutiae. |
| **B** | 7–8 | The methods are communicated clearly. Key tools and the general approach are identified. The level of detail is appropriate, with minor omissions or imbalances that do not obscure the overall approach. A visual aid may be present but not optimally designed. |
| **C** | 6 | The methods are described but the description is either too vague (the examiner cannot understand the approach) or too detailed (the student reads through a list of every step). Key elements are identifiable but the pipeline is not communicated as a coherent workflow. |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | The methods are poorly communicated. Key tools or databases are not identified, or the description is so brief as to be meaningless. The examiner cannot determine how the analysis was conducted. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | Methods are absent from the presentation, or the description is incoherent. No meaningful understanding of the analytical approach can be gained from the presentation. |

---

### A3. Results (15 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 13–15 | Results are presented using clear, well-chosen visual outputs (figures, graphs, structural visualisations, etc.). The narration leads with the finding, not the method. Findings are selected judiciously — the student presents the most important results rather than attempting to cover everything. Quantitative findings are cited precisely. Each result is directly connected to the research question. The examiner is left with a clear understanding of what was found. |
| **B** | 11–12 | Key results are clearly communicated and supported by appropriate visual aids. The narration generally leads with the finding. Selection of results is mostly appropriate. Minor issues with precision, visual quality, or relevance to the research question do not significantly detract from the overall presentation of findings. |
| **C** | 8–10 | Results are presented and the main findings can be understood. Visual aids may be present but poorly designed, difficult to read, or not fully explained in the narration. The student may attempt to present too many results superficially, or key figures may be read rather than explained. The examiner can identify the main findings but with some effort. |
| **D / D-** | 6–7 | Results are incomplete, unclear, or dominated by descriptions of what was done rather than what was found. Visual aids may be absent, illegible, or not referred to. The examiner cannot clearly identify the main findings of the project. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | Results are absent or cannot be understood from the presentation. Findings are not communicated. Slides may contain raw data outputs with no narration or context. |

---

### A4. Discussion and Conclusions (15 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 13–15 | The student interprets their findings confidently and accurately. They explain what the results mean in the context of the research question and in relation to the broader field. At least one significant limitation is honestly identified. The take-home message is clear, memorable, and directly addresses the original research question. The examiner leaves the presentation with a strong understanding of what was found, what it means, and why it matters. |
| **B** | 11–12 | Key findings are interpreted accurately and the significance is explained. The take-home message is clear. A limitation is noted. The discussion may lack depth or nuance in one area (e.g., connection to literature, specificity of limitation) but is overall convincing and appropriately delivered within the time constraints. |
| **C** | 8–10 | Conclusions are present and broadly accurate. Interpretation may be superficial or may restate results rather than interpret them. A take-home message is identifiable. Limitation may be generic or absent. The examiner understands what the student concludes but the discussion is not fully convincing. |
| **D / D-** | 6–7 | The discussion is very brief, superficial, or missing. Conclusions may be vague, overstated, or not clearly linked to the findings presented. The take-home message is unclear. No limitation is identified. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | Discussion and conclusions are absent or incoherent. The student does not demonstrate any ability to interpret their own findings. |

## Component B: Slide Design and Visual Communication (20 marks)

*This component assesses the quality of the slides as a visual communication tool. In professional practice, the ability to design clear, effective slides is as important as the ability to speak. Slides are assessed on their role in supporting the presentation — they should aid understanding, not replace the narration or distract from it.*

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 17–20 | Slides are professionally designed with a consistent, clean visual style. Each slide has a clear purpose and a meaningful headline title. Figures and visualisations are of high quality, clearly labelled, and directly relevant to the narration. Text on slides is minimal and used only to support key points — the slides do not contain the student's script. The visual design enhances the communication of the science. The overall aesthetic is polished and appropriate to a professional scientific presentation. |
| **B** | 14–16 | Slides are well-designed and clearly support the presentation. Figures and labels are clear. Headline titles are mostly effective. Text may be slightly excessive in one or two places. The visual quality is good overall with minor inconsistencies or design weaknesses that do not significantly impede communication. |
| **C** | 11–13 | Slides are functional and the main content is conveyed, but design weaknesses are apparent. Slides may contain too much text. Some figures may be difficult to read (small labels, low contrast, poor resolution). Titles may be generic rather than informative. The overall design does not actively hinder the presentation but does not enhance it either. |
| **D / D-** | 8–10 | Slides have significant design weaknesses that interfere with communication. Heavy use of text; little or no use of visual aids in results. Figures may be illegible or unlabelled. Inconsistent styling. The examiner has to work to understand the slides. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–7 | Slides are of very poor quality or nearly absent in content. Results are presented as text only or raw software output. The visual presentation actively hinders understanding. |

## Component C: Verbal Delivery (20 marks)

*This component assesses how effectively the student communicates verbally. It explicitly recognises the diversity of the student cohort. Students are not assessed on accent, native-level fluency, or speaking style — they are assessed on whether their delivery effectively communicates the scientific content to the listener. A student who speaks clearly and accurately in English as a second language can achieve a Distinction in this component.*

*Sub-components: Clarity and pace (10 marks); Engagement and confidence (10 marks).*

---

### C1. Clarity and Pace (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | The narration is clearly audible throughout. Speech is at a pace that allows the listener to follow without effort — neither rushed nor excessively slow. Technical terms are pronounced with sufficient clarity to be understood. The narration flows naturally with the slide content. The listener can follow the entire presentation without needing to replay sections. |
| **B** | 7–8 | The narration is clearly audible and understandable. Pace is mostly appropriate with occasional moments of rushing or slowing. Technical terms are generally clear. A listener may need to concentrate to follow one or two passages but the overall delivery is effective. |
| **C** | 6 | The narration is understandable for most of the presentation but clarity or pace issues are present. The listener may need to replay one or more sections to follow the content. Pacing may be consistently too fast or too slow. Technical terms may occasionally be unclear. *Note: Students speaking in English as an additional language who demonstrate genuine effort to speak clearly and at an appropriate pace will not be penalised for pronunciation patterns associated with their first language, provided the content is understandable.* |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | Significant clarity or pace issues throughout. The listener must frequently work hard to understand the narration. Long inaudible passages, very rushed delivery, or very monotone delivery that makes it hard to follow the argument. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | The narration is substantially inaudible or incomprehensible throughout. The presentation cannot be assessed from the audio. |

---

### C2. Engagement and Confidence (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | The student presents with evident confidence and conviction in their material. The narration sounds natural and engaged, not read verbatim from a script. The student varies their tone and emphasis appropriately, slowing for key findings and signalling transitions between sections. The listener is left with the impression that the student genuinely understands and cares about their work. |
| **B** | 7–8 | The student presents with reasonable confidence. The narration is mostly natural, with occasional passages that sound scripted but do not significantly detract from the overall effect. Emphasis and variation are present, if not consistently applied. |
| **C** | 6 | The narration is largely read from a script in a flat tone, reducing engagement. However, the content is communicated and the student demonstrates basic familiarity with their material. Some variation in tone or emphasis may be present. The presentation lacks energy but is not ineffective. |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | The narration is read verbatim and in a monotone throughout. It is unclear whether the student understands the material or is simply reading text prepared by themselves or others. Engagement is very low. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | The narration demonstrates no engagement with the content. The student may be reading text they do not understand, or the delivery is so flat and scripted that it cannot be assessed as a presentation. |

---

## Component D: Structure and Timing (10 marks)

*This component assesses whether the presentation follows a logical, coherent structure and whether the student has managed their time effectively. Both are professional competencies assessed by this format.*

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A** | 9–10 | The presentation follows a clear, logical structure from introduction through to conclusion. Each section transitions naturally to the next. Time is managed effectively: the presentation is within ±15 seconds of 5 minutes. Time is distributed proportionally — the student does not spend 3 minutes on background and rush through results. The narrative is coherent as a whole. |
| **B** | 7–8 | The structure is clear and logical. Transitions are mostly smooth. Timing is within ±30 seconds. One section may be slightly over- or under-weighted relative to its importance, but the overall pacing is appropriate. |
| **C** | 6 | A basic structure is identifiable (introduction, methods, results, conclusions). Some transitions are abrupt or missing. Timing is within ±45 seconds, or one section is noticeably disproportionate (e.g., excessive time on methods, rushed conclusions). The overall flow is adequate but could be significantly improved. |
| **D / D-** | 4–5 | Structure is unclear or disorganised. Sections may be missing or in a confusing order. Timing is significantly off (more than 45 seconds over or under), or time distribution is severely disproportionate (e.g., almost no time for results). |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | No discernible structure. The presentation cannot be followed as a coherent narrative. Timing is so far from the requirement as to suggest the student did not rehearse or prepare adequately. |

## Guidance Notes for Examiners

**Timing:** Record the exact duration of the presentation. Presentations over 5:15 or under 4:45 should have marks deducted within Component D, as specified by that component's descriptors. Do not award a Distinction in Component D for presentations outside ±15 seconds.

**Audio failure:** If the embedded audio does not play and you cannot open the file after reasonable effort, contact the module coordinator before marking. Do not award zero without first attempting to resolve the technical issue, in case the failure is on the examiner's device.

**Scientific content vs. delivery:** Components A and B/C/D assess largely independent skills. A presentation with strong scientific content but weak delivery should score well on A and less well on B/C/D. Do not allow excellent delivery to inflate the scientific content score, and do not allow weak delivery to deflate it.

**Language equity:** Component C1 and C2 descriptors explicitly accommodate second-language English speakers. Penalise communication failures (content cannot be understood), not accent or non-native grammatical constructions in the narration, provided meaning is clear.

**Second examiner review:** All submissions awarded a grade of D/D- or below, or A/A+, should be reviewed by a second examiner before grades are released, in accordance with UCD assessment regulations.
