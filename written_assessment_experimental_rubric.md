# CHEN40140 Biopharmaceutical Engineering Project
## MEngSc in Biopharmaceutical Engineering — Summer Research Project
## Written Assessment: Grading Rubric
### Full Research Thesis
### Experimental Projects Only

---

> **Scope:** This rubric applies exclusively to **experimental summer research projects** involving laboratory work. Examiners should assess Materials and Methods sections for the detail and reproducibility of experimental protocols, and Results sections for the quality of experimental data, including the honest reporting of optimisation steps and negative or inconclusive results. This rubric does not apply to bioinformatics projects, which are assessed under a separate rubric.

The rubric is divided into **four assessed components**, each weighted as shown. Within each component, five performance levels are defined — corresponding to the five broad grade bands above. The per-component mark ranges are calibrated so that a student consistently scoring at the bottom of a given band will receive a total mark that falls within the corresponding grade range on the Alternative Linear Conversion Scale.

## Component A: Scientific Content and Critical Thinking (50 marks)

*This component assesses whether the student understands their own project: the hypothesis or objectives, the experimental approach, the findings, and their meaning. It is the most heavily weighted component because the ability to conduct and communicate rigorous experimental research is the primary learning outcome of this project.*

*Sub-components: Hypothesis/objectives (10 marks); Materials and Methods (10 marks); Results (15 marks); Discussion and conclusions (15 marks).*

---

### A1. Hypothesis / Objectives (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A / A-** | 9–10 | The project hypothesis or objectives are precisely stated and logically motivated by the preceding introduction. The biological/engineering significance is convincingly established. The reader is left in no doubt about what the project set out to test or achieve and why it matters. The scope of the project is clearly defined. |
| **B+ / B / B-** | 7–8 | The hypothesis or objectives are clearly stated and well-motivated. The significance of the work is established with good use of context. Minor improvements in precision or framing are possible but the overall rationale is sound. |
| **C+ / C / C-** | 6 | A hypothesis or set of objectives is present and broadly understandable. The rationale is given but may be somewhat generic or not fully specific to the study. The aim may be stated in general terms. The reader understands what was done but the "why" is not fully convincing. |
| **D+ / D / D-** | 4–5 | A hypothesis or objective is implied but not clearly articulated. The significance is described in very general terms. The aim may be confused with the methods or results. Considerable improvement needed. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | No clear hypothesis or objectives can be identified. The introduction does not establish why the work was done or what it sought to achieve. Fundamental misunderstanding of the project's purpose is evident, or the section is absent. |

---

### A2. Materials and Methods (10 marks)

*This component assesses the reproducibility and rigour of the experimental methodology. The Methods section of an experimental thesis must enable another competent researcher to reproduce the experiments described. This is a core requirement of scientific reporting.*

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A / A-** | 9–10 | The methods are described with precision and completeness. All materials (reagents, cell lines, media) are identified with suppliers and relevant specifications. Equipment is named with manufacturer and model where appropriate. Experimental protocols are described step-by-step with all key parameters (concentrations, temperatures, durations, replication). Statistical methods are clearly stated. A competent researcher could reproduce all experiments from this description alone. Where methods were optimised, the optimisation process is documented. |
| **B+ / B / B-** | 7–8 | The methods are largely complete and accurate. Key materials and protocols are described with sufficient detail for broad reproducibility. Minor omissions (e.g., a supplier name, a centrifugation speed) do not fundamentally undermine reproducibility. Statistical methods are stated. The methods section is logically organised. |
| **C+ / C / C-** | 6 | The methods describe what was done at a general level. Major materials and techniques are mentioned but details are incomplete — a reader could understand the general approach but could not reproduce specific experiments. Some protocols lack key parameters. Statistical methods may be vague or absent. |
| **D+ / D / D-** | 4–5 | The methods are superficial or disorganised. Important materials, equipment, or protocols may be missing. The description reads more like a general narrative than a reproducible experimental protocol. Key decisions about experimental design are not explained. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | The methods section is absent, incoherent, or so incomplete as to be scientifically meaningless. It is not possible to determine what experiments were performed. |

---

### A3. Results (15 marks)

*This component assesses the quality, completeness, and presentation of experimental data. It explicitly values the honest reporting of negative results and experimental optimisation, which are integral to laboratory research.*

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A / A-** | 13–15 | Results are presented with clarity, precision, and appropriate scientific rigour. Data are quantified with appropriate statistics (means, standard deviations, significance tests). All figures and tables are of high quality, correctly labelled, and have informative, self-contained legends. The results are organised logically (not necessarily chronologically) into chapters that each address a clear question. Negative or inconclusive results are reported honestly and discussed. Optimisation steps are documented where relevant, showing the student's practical problem-solving. The student demonstrates excellent judgement in what to present and how. |
| **B+ / B / B-** | 11–12 | Results are clearly presented with appropriate use of figures and tables. Key findings are identifiable and quantified. Negative results are reported. The organisation is logical with minor gaps in flow. Some figures or tables could be improved. Statistical reporting is mostly appropriate with minor omissions. The overall presentation is sound. |
| **C+ / C / C-** | 8–10 | Results are present and the main findings can be understood. Figures and tables are included but may have deficiencies (incomplete legends, inconsistent labelling, missing error bars or replication data). Some findings are described in vague terms. Negative results may be mentioned but not adequately discussed. The organisation is adequate but could be improved. Some results may be presented without appropriate statistical analysis. |
| **D+ / D / D-** | 6–7 | Results are incomplete, disorganised, or difficult to follow. Key findings may be missing or described so vaguely as to be uninformative. Figures may be absent, poorly labelled, or not referred to in the text. Negative results are not reported. The distinction between results and discussion is blurred. Statistical analysis is largely absent. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | Results are absent, incoherent, or consist of raw data with no narrative or analysis. There is no meaningful communication of what was found. |

---

### A4. Discussion and Conclusions (15 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A / A-** | 13–15 | The discussion demonstrates genuine critical thinking. Key findings — including negative results — are interpreted confidently and correctly in the context of the research hypothesis/objectives. Literature is actively engaged: the student explains how their findings compare to, extend, or challenge prior work. Limitations are identified with specificity and intellectual honesty (e.g., acknowledging limited replicates, time constraints, equipment limitations). Conclusions are precise, directly answer the stated hypothesis/objectives, and are fully supported by the results. Recommendations for future work are specific and well-reasoned. The student demonstrates scientific maturity. |
| **B+ / B / B-** | 11–12 | Key findings are interpreted accurately and contextualised with relevant literature. The discussion is logically structured. Limitations are acknowledged meaningfully. Conclusions are clear and supported. Recommendations for future work are present and reasonable. Some scope for deeper critical engagement. |
| **C+ / C / C-** | 8–10 | The discussion interprets main findings but may be somewhat descriptive or superficial. Literature references are included but engagement is limited. Limitations are acknowledged at a general level. Conclusions are present but may be overstated, understated, or not clearly linked to the original hypothesis/objectives. Recommendations for future work may be generic. |
| **D+ / D / D-** | 6–7 | The discussion largely restates results without interpretation. Critical engagement with the literature is minimal or absent. Limitations are not addressed. Conclusions are vague, unsupported, or contradict the results. No meaningful recommendations for future work. Evidence of significant misunderstanding of the data. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | The discussion is absent, incoherent, or cannot be distinguished from the results. There is no meaningful interpretation of findings. Conclusions are absent or fundamentally incorrect. |

## Component B: Structure and Adherence to Thesis Format (15 marks)

*This component assesses whether the student has followed the prescribed thesis format (as specified in the Research Project Guidelines) and whether the document is logically organised.*

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A / A-** | 13–15 | All required sections are present, correctly ordered, and appropriately proportioned. The title page, abstract, acknowledgements, nomenclature, lists of figures/tables, and appendices all meet specifications. The abstract effectively covers the four required elements within the page limit. Chapters are logically structured and clearly titled. The thesis reads as a coherent, professionally structured document. Word count is within the 20,000-word limit. Page numbering, margins, and formatting are correct throughout. |
| **B+ / B / B-** | 11–12 | All required sections are present and correctly ordered. The abstract is mostly effective. Minor issues with proportionality between chapters or minor formatting inconsistencies do not detract significantly. The thesis is well-organised overall with minor structural weaknesses. |
| **C+ / C / C-** | 8–10 | All major sections are present but one or more may be poorly proportioned, incomplete, or missing expected content (e.g., nomenclature list absent, figure list incomplete). The abstract may have significant omissions. The document is recognisable as a thesis but structural weaknesses are apparent. |
| **D+ / D / D-** | 6–7 | One or more required sections are missing or so minimal as to be ineffective. The structure deviates substantially from the prescribed format. Chapters may lack clear organisation. Word count may substantially exceed the limit. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–5 | The submission does not follow the prescribed thesis format. Required sections are absent, scrambled, or unlabelled. The document does not resemble a research thesis. |

## Component C: Written Communication (20 marks)

*This component assesses the clarity, concision, and professional quality of the student's writing. It explicitly recognises that students write in English as a second or third language. The standard assessed is appropriate academic scientific writing — not native-speaker fluency. Grammatical errors that do not impede understanding are penalised less heavily than structural or clarity issues.*

*Sub-components: Clarity and precision of expression (10 marks); Grammar, spelling, and academic register (10 marks).*

---

### C1. Clarity and Precision of Expression (10 marks)

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| **A+ / A / A-** | 9–10 | Writing is consistently clear, precise, and well-constructed. Technical concepts are explained at the appropriate level. Paragraphs are cohesive and transitions between ideas are logical. The student writes with confidence and authority. Information density is high without being difficult to parse. |
| **B+ / B / B-** | 7–8 | Writing is mostly clear and precise. Occasional sentences are ambiguous or unnecessarily complex, but these do not significantly impede understanding. The overall argument is easy to follow. |
| **C+ / C / C-** | 6 | The writing is generally understandable but clarity is inconsistent. Some passages are vague, overly complex, or poorly structured. Technical terms may be used imprecisely. The overall meaning can be extracted with effort. |
| **D+ / D / D-** | 4–5 | Significant clarity issues throughout. Many passages are difficult to understand. Vague or imprecise language is frequent. The reader must work hard to extract meaning. |
| **Fail (E+ and below)** | 0–3 | The writing is largely incomprehensible or so poorly structured that the content cannot be assessed. |

| Band | Mark Range | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|

## Guidance Notes for Examiners

- **Word count**: The 20,000-word limit is a maximum. Do not penalise concise theses that cover all necessary content. Theses that significantly exceed 20,000 words should have 5 marks deducted from Component B.
- **Negative results**: Students who honestly report and thoughtfully discuss negative or inconclusive results should be credited in Component A3 and A4. The absence of negative results in a thesis that clearly involved experimental troubleshooting should raise questions.
- **Optimisation**: Experimental projects often require iterative optimisation. Evidence that the student understood their experimental system well enough to troubleshoot and refine protocols is a positive indicator for Components A2 and A3.
- **Academic integrity**: Where Turnitin flags concerns, refer to the UCD Academic Integrity Policy (effective 1 September 2024) before completing marking. Do not award marks to content that appears to have been generated by AI or copied without attribution.
- **Language equity**: Component C2 descriptors explicitly accommodate second-language writers. Grammatical patterns consistent with a specific first-language background should not be penalised if meaning is clear.
- **Second examiner review**: All submissions awarded a grade of D/D- or below, or A/A+, should be reviewed by a second examiner before grades are released, in accordance with UCD assessment regulations.
